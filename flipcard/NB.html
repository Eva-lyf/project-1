Niel’s Bohr Hydrogenic Atoms Model

To explain the line spectra emitted by atoms, as well as
the stability of atoms, Niel’s Bohr proposed a model for hydrogenic (single elctron) atoms. He introduced three postulates and laid the foundations of quantum mechanics:


  • (a) In a hydrogen atom, an electron revolves in
    certain stable orbits (called stationary orbits)
    without the emission of radiant energy.

  • (b) The stationary orbits are those for which the angular momentum is some integral multiple of
    h/2p. (Bohr’s quantisation condition.) That is
    L = nh/2p, where n is an integer called the
    principal quantum number.

  • (c) The third postulate states that an electron might make a transition from one of its specified non-radiating orbits to another of lower energy. When it does so, a photon is emitted having energy equal to the energy difference between the initial and final states. The frequency "n" of the emitted photon is then given by hn = Ei – Ef An atom absorbs radiation of the same frequency the atom emits, in which case the electron is transferred to an orbit with a higher value of n. Ei + hn = Ef.
